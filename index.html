While the force of gravity is strong, boomerangs have a special talent. Due to a boomerangâ€™s typically thin, yet broad surface area, it is able to remain in the air successfully. And as they are flying through the air, boomerangs fly parallel to the ground and maintain a sufficient rotation and speed. By doing this, a boomerang can resist the force of gravity.

Boomerangs have two wings that are arranged with the leading edges faced in the same direction, similar to blades of a propeller. When these wings spin, they create a force that spins the entire boomerang through the air. This force turns into a forward motion as the wings lift higher into the air.

As the boomerang is thrown, there is a force applied on both opposite ends of the wood. This opposing force balances out the initial motion of the throw. The air acts as another force, pushing on top of the boomerang and counterbalancing the opposing forces to keep the throw steady.

The tilt of the wings is also important in understanding how a boomerang works. A boomerang should be thrown vertically, creating an outward and upward tilt. Thus, air can easily pass over the top curved wing of the boomerang forcing it to go faster than the air under the bottom wing. This creates less pressure above the top wing, resulting in an upward lift.

The speed of the wings creates an effect known as precession. How? The upper wing of a boomerang turns in one direction of motion, while the lower wing turns in the opposite direction. This results in the upper wing moving faster than the other wing subjecting the lower wing to turn in a circular motion all the way back to the starting location.
